# The Life of Souls
*By Nandhagopal*

## The Great Race

Have you ever wondered why souls work the way they do? Or even more simply: *What am I?* In this story, I explore the fictional journey of souls — how they are crafted and how they transition into our reality. Our lives are scripted and calculated by nature long before we arrive.

From the very beginning, we are fighters. To even reach the threshold of human life, we must surpass billions of others in a frantic race. Our biological origin — the sperm — is essentially a clone of our potential, fighting to win the ultimate prize: **existence**.

---

## The Mechanics of the Game

To win, we are connected to the **"World of Souls,"** where we must constantly surpass our own limits. Life often reveals its true nature when there is no one left to talk to; in those moments, you realize that life is a sophisticated game.

In this moral world, there are billions of "games" occurring simultaneously. You choose your path, you join the fray, and you play to win. But through it all, there is only you. It has always been you. You can play fair, you can try to cheat, or you can forge your own path — but we all get our chances. We know the probability of success, and we must either play our hand or step aside.

> Whether you choose to watch or play, you must understand one thing: **Pain is gain.** Without struggle, there is no true growth.

---

## The Law of Return

Our journeys are well-defined: what you give, you receive in return — often twofold. If you put out hate, you receive hate. You know yourself better than anyone else, yet the Universe (or God) knows you better than you know yourself.

> *"Be water, my friend."*

Stop comparing yourself to others; they are simply different versions of a soul — some further along the path, some just starting. Water can adapt to any vessel and overcome any obstacle.

---

## The Lobby and the Legacy

While we play, other versions of souls are waiting in the **"game lobby."** This is the cycle of life. We pass our hard-earned knowledge to the younger generations, and they follow the patterns we leave behind. When one player loses the game or their time ends, a new "gamer" enters the arena.

If the game isn't hard, why would we play? When things get easy, we naturally seek a higher difficulty level. We are all separated by our individual bodies, yet we remain **forever united by nature**.
